Introduction

How to use this playbook

A reference for the moments when you need it — not a document to read cover to cover.

This playbook exists because data alone doesn't produce insight, and insight alone doesn't produce action. The communications team now has a framework, a set of tools, and a reporting rhythm. This playbook is the companion that helps you use them well — specifically, the parts that require skill and judgment rather than just following a process.

Every section is written to be used in the moment. Pull it up when you're stuck, when you're leading a team through an ICT activity, or when you need a quick reminder of how to approach something. You don't need to have read anything else in this playbook to use any section of it.

The Reporting Rhythm This Playbook Supports

Every section in this guide maps to a specific moment in the cycle below. The ICT editorial meeting is the heartbeat; quarterly planning sets the frame; monthly reporting is the output. Click to expand.

For working sessions

The ICT meeting lead can open a section during Week 3 or Week 4 to guide the team through story candidate identification or narrative hypothesis drafting.

For individual reference

Jump directly to what you need. If you're writing a story tile and stuck on the "why it matters," go to Crafting a compelling narrative. If you're not sure whether a metric is a story, go to What makes a story.

For onboarding

New team members can read the Foundations and Planning sections front-to-back to build context, then use the rest as reference once they're working within the rhythm.

What this isn't

This is not a user guide for the dashboard or the Excel workbook — those have their own documentation. This is about developing the thinking and communication skills behind the reporting.

How each section is structured

Every topic follows the same template: When to use this tells you exactly which moment the section is for. The core idea gives you the concept in plain language. How to do it is the numbered practical guide. Example shows it applied to real communications reporting scenarios. Watch out for flags the most common mistakes.

Foundations

What makes a story

Not every metric is a story. Here's how to tell the difference.

When to use this

When you're trying to decide whether something that happened this month is worth reporting on — or when you're reviewing a draft and wondering whether it's telling a story or just listing numbers.

The core idea

A story answers four questions: something changed or happened, it matters to someone in a position to act on it, it connects to a decision or strategic priority, and it points toward a next step. Data tells you what happened. A story tells you why it matters and what to do about it. Most comms reporting delivers the first without the others.

The fastest test: Would this change a decision, or inform a judgment, that someone with authority needs to make? If the answer is yes — even partially — you likely have a story. If the answer is "it shows we did things," you have data.

How to do it
  1. Name what changed or happened. Start with the observation, grounded in a specific metric and a time period. "Our LinkedIn engagement rate was 3.8% in May, up 7% month-over-month."
  2. Ask: Is this change meaningful? Is it outside normal variance? Part of a longer trend? Connected to something deliberate you did? A one-month spike isn't a story; a sustained shift above target might be.
  3. Connect it to a quarterly priority. Which theme from the Quarterly Communications Plan does this support? If it doesn't connect to a priority, it may still be data worth tracking — but it's harder to justify elevating it to the executive report.
  4. Ask: Who cares, and what would they do differently? If you can't name a stakeholder and a decision this informs, the story isn't ready yet.
  5. Ask: What would you recommend? If you can't answer this, you have information — not yet an insight. Keep working.
Example

"Internal Benefits Enrollment emails had a 4.2% CTR in May." That's data.

"The Internal Benefits Enrollment email has underperformed its 4.9% CTR target for three consecutive months, despite stable open rates — suggesting the subject lines are working but the content isn't driving action, and a format and CTA review is needed before the July enrollment deadline." That's a story. It's specific, it has a pattern (three months), it names a consequence (enrollment deadline), and it implies an owned action.

Watch out for
  • The quantity trap. More data points don't make a stronger story — they make a harder-to-read one.
  • The novelty bias. Assuming a new metric is automatically interesting. It isn't until it has context.
  • The "we did things" report. Listing activities (pitches sent, events hosted, posts published) without stating what they achieved is not a story.

The tiered metrics framework in practice

Three tiers, three audiences, three different conversations.

When to use this

When you're deciding which metrics belong in the executive report versus the dashboard versus team-level monitoring — or when someone asks you to add a metric to the leadership report and you're not sure whether it belongs there.

The core idea

The three tiers aren't just a hierarchy — they answer three different questions for three different audiences. Tier 1 is for leadership: what did communications achieve for the business? Tier 2 is for the comms team: how did communications activity drive those outcomes? Tier 3 is for channel leads: what was produced, and how efficiently?

The most common error is elevating T3 activity metrics (posts published, emails sent, events hosted) into executive reporting. Volume is not value. Leadership cares about what the volume achieved — and that's a T1 or T2 question.

How to do it
  1. When a metric comes up, ask: Who needs to act on this information? If it's the CMO, CCO, or a business unit leader: likely T1. If it's a communications director: T2. If it's a channel lead or individual contributor: T3.
  2. Executive reports contain T1 metrics only, with T2 metrics used selectively as evidence for a story narrative. "Our reputation score held at 71/100 despite the safety communications challenge — earned media sentiment remaining at 78% positive was a key driver." T1 leads; T2 supports.
  3. The dashboard gives the full picture — T1 on the overview, T2 on detail pages, T3 in tables. Direct anyone who wants more detail there rather than adding it to the exec report.
  4. When asked to add a metric to the executive report, ask: "Does this tell leadership something about business impact, or about what the team did?" If the latter, it belongs in T2 or T3.
  5. Use this question as a tiebreaker: "Would the CCO change a decision or priority based on this number, or would they simply note it?" T1 = yes. T2/T3 = probably not directly.
Example

T2 being mistaken for T1: Town Hall Attendance Rate (84%) is a T2 metric — it tells us internal communication reached its audience at high rates. The T1 metric is Employee Vision Score — whether that attendance actually improved employees' understanding of company direction. You'd cite attendance in the executive report as evidence supporting an EVS story, not as the story itself.

T3 creeping into executive reporting: "We published 87 social posts in May." This is T3. What those 87 posts achieved (engagement rate, narrative presence, advocacy reach) is T2 and T1. Report the outcome, not the output.

Watch out for
  • T3 creep. Pressure from teams to "show our work" leads to volume metrics appearing in leadership reports. This reduces the report's impact and makes it harder for leadership to see what matters.
  • Confusing T1 and T2. Reputation Score is T1 (business outcome). Earned media sentiment is T2 (comms outcome contributing to reputation). One informs the other, but they're not interchangeable.
  • Using T2 without the T1 story. A T2 metric in isolation (without the T1 context it supports) is just data. Always pair T2 evidence with the T1 story it's telling.

Planning

Running the quarterly kick-off

The meeting that makes all monthly reporting intentional.

When to use this

One to two weeks before the start of each quarter. This is the planning session that produces the Quarterly Communications Plan — the document that anchors all ICT agendas and monthly reporting for the next three months.

The core idea

The quarterly kick-off is the meeting that prevents monthly reporting from being reactive. Everything in the reporting system — the ICT additions, the monthly narrative, the executive report framing — refers back to what was decided here. If you skip it or rush it, reporting loses its through-line and reverts to assembling data after the fact.

The output is a completed Quarterly Communications Plan: a one-page document defining the quarter's priority themes, key stories, channel assignments, success hypotheses, key dates, and known gaps.

How to do it
  1. Review the prior quarter's results. Which themes performed? What did leadership respond to most strongly? What underperformed, and why? This gives the new quarter's plan an evidence base rather than starting from a blank page.
  2. Confirm 2–3 priority themes for the quarter from the five evergreen themes. More than three themes means no story gets the attention it needs.
  3. For each theme, identify the key stories or campaigns already planned or in market. Assign channel ownership for each story — a named lead, not a team.
  4. Write the success hypothesis for each theme. The format: "If we execute this effectively, we expect [specific T1 or T2 metric] to [direction and magnitude]." Quantitative where baselines exist. (See the next section if baselines are new.)
  5. Identify the quarter's key dates and milestones — corporate events, product launches, earnings, anything that will shape content planning.
  6. Flag known gaps. Which metrics are still being established? Where is methodology TBD? Get these on paper now so they don't surprise you mid-quarter.
Example — Q3 2026 success hypothesis

Theme: Innovation, R&D & AI (primary)

Weak hypothesis: "We expect strong narrative presence in the innovation theme."

Strong hypothesis: "Priority Narrative Presence in top-tier coverage ≥ 72% (current baseline: 68%; target: +4pts MoM in at least 2 of 3 months). Organic Social ER ≥ 3.8% on innovation-tagged content. GenAI Search Share of Voice reaches 40% by end of Q3 (current: 34%)."

The strong version names the metric, the current baseline, the target, and the time frame. When October's reporting comes around, there's no ambiguity about whether Q3 was successful.

Watch out for
  • Skipping the prior-quarter review. Without it, each quarter starts from zero and the reporting loses its longitudinal story.
  • Setting too many themes. More than three means every story competes for attention. The monthly report will feel scattered.
  • Vague success hypotheses. "Improve brand perception" is not a hypothesis — it's a direction. A hypothesis is testable: you can look back at the end of the quarter and say definitively whether you achieved it.

Planning

Setting targets & baselines

What to do when you don't have historical data — and how to set targets that are honest.

When to use this

During the quarterly kick-off when writing success hypotheses, or whenever a stakeholder asks "is that a good number?" and you need to give a considered answer rather than a guess.

The core idea

A target without a baseline is a guess, and a guess is a credibility risk. In the first year of using this framework, several metrics won't have historical data — Employee Vision Score, the OEI components, CVOI. That's fine and expected. The answer is to be explicit about it: designate the current period as the baseline, state what you're establishing, and set the target once you have something to stand on.

Trying to set ambitious targets without evidence, or avoiding targets altogether because baselines are missing, are both mistakes. Transparency about where you are in the data maturity curve is more credible than false precision.

How to do it
  1. Check: do you have any historical data? Even three months is a usable baseline. If yes, move to step 2. If no, jump to step 4.
  2. Set a target based on trend or benchmark. Trend: "We've averaged 68% over the last three quarters; target is to maintain or improve to 70% this quarter." Benchmark: "Industry median for this metric is 65; our target is to stay above it."
  3. Document the logic. Note in the Quarterly Communications Plan why the target is what it is. This prevents "why did we target 72%?" questions three months later.
  4. If no baseline exists: name the current period as the baseline quarter. Write in the QCP: "[Metric] — Q3 2026 is our baseline period. Target to be set following Q3 results." This is not a gap — it's honest sequencing.
  5. After each month, revisit. Is the target realistic? Are you measuring the right thing? A target that's never challenged is either too easy or being ignored.
Example — Employee Vision Score

The EVS doesn't have a baseline yet. Setting a target of "75%" with no evidence creates a risk: if Q1 comes in at 68%, does that mean the quarter failed? Not necessarily — 68% might be a strong baseline for an organization this size. You don't know yet.

Better approach for Q3: "Employee Vision Score — Q3 2026 is our baseline quarter. We will set a target at the start of Q4 based on the Q3 result." When Q3 comes in at 68%, you set Q4's target: "Maintain or improve from 68% baseline; target ≥ 70% by year-end." Now you have a target with a rationale.

Watch out for
  • Aspirational targets without a path. "We'll reach 80% by Q4" with no plan for how gets you there creates a credibility problem when you report 71%.
  • Assuming high = good for every metric. For some metrics, stability is the goal. Issue/Crisis Containment at zero escalations is perfect; it shouldn't "improve" from there.
  • Not revisiting targets mid-year. If something material changes (methodology, scope, team structure), the target may need to change too. Document the reason when you adjust.

The ICT Cycle

Story candidate identification

The W3 skill: looking at what happened and identifying what might be worth telling.

The Full Four-Week ICT Cycle

Story candidate identification is the W3 step. The diagram below shows how it fits within the full weekly rotation — and how the W4 narrative hypothesis step that follows connects to the monthly reporting cadence. Click to expand.

When to use this

Week 3 of the ICT meeting cycle — after reviewing the social listening dashboard. This is where you collectively look at what's been in market and surface 2–4 story candidates to carry into the W4 narrative hypothesis session.

The core idea

In Week 3, you don't have complete data. You're working from observation, instinct, and what people closest to the work are noticing in the field. The goal is a short list of candidates — not conclusions, just hypotheses worth pursuing. Think of it as an editorial meeting: what happened this month that might matter?

The most important distinction to hold: story candidate ≠ confirmed story. You are identifying potential, not claiming outcomes. The data will confirm or challenge these candidates in early Month N+1.

How to do it
  1. Brief round-table: each channel lead shares 1–2 observations. Not metrics — observations. "The LinkedIn series seemed to generate more organic commentary than usual." "Town hall Q&A was notably longer and more engaged than prior sessions." "Benefits emails are being opened but something seems to be blocking action."
  2. Cross-reference against the quarterly plan. Which candidates connect to a priority theme? Candidates with a theme connection have a clearer path to the executive report.
  3. Score each candidate on three dimensions: Was it intentional (planned campaign vs. incidental)? Does it connect to a strategic priority? Would it change a leadership decision or inform their view? Highest scores go forward.
  4. Select 2–4 candidates to carry into W4. More than four makes the W4 session unwieldy, and you'll only tell 2–3 in the final report anyway.
  5. Assign an owner for each candidate who will come to W4 prepared to draft the narrative hypothesis.
Example — May W3 session

Three candidates surface in the round-table:

1. Sustainability Campaign / Innovation theme: Channel lead notes the LinkedIn two-part series is getting organic shares and comments well above normal. Connects to Q3 Innovation priority. Goes forward.

2. Q1 Town Hall Series close: HR comms lead says attendance was strong and Q&A ran 20 minutes over — "it felt like a different kind of engagement." Connects to Community Impact theme. Goes forward.

3. Benefits Enrollment emails: FirstUp lead notices open rates are stable but something about the conversion feels off. Not connected to a priority theme, but it's a watch item with a real deadline. Goes forward.

A fourth observation (the number of press releases published this month) gets noted for the T3 table but doesn't become a story candidate — it's volume, not outcome.

Watch out for
  • Treating candidates as confirmed stories. W3 is for observation. Claims and conclusions come after data is available.
  • Letting the loudest voice dominate. Stories about big, visible campaigns aren't automatically more important than quieter stories with genuine business impact. A small but meaningful shift in employee engagement can matter more than a large media push.
  • Selecting too many candidates. Limit to 2–4. The W4 session needs to be focused, not a marathon.

The ICT Cycle

Narrative hypothesis drafting

The W4 skill: forming a testable story before the data arrives.

When to use this

Week 4 of the ICT meeting cycle — the monthly wrap-up. You're taking the story candidates from W3 and drafting initial hypotheses about what each story would say if the data confirms what you observed.

The core idea

The hypothesis is a testable statement: "If the data shows what we think it shows, here's the story we'd tell." It's explicitly not the final narrative — it's a starting point that gets refined against actual data in early Month N+1. Its value is speed: when the data arrives, you're refining rather than starting from scratch.

Think of it as the difference between a photographer who sees the shot before pressing the shutter versus one who presses the shutter and hopes something good appears. The hypothesis is the eye before the data is the light.

How to do it
  1. For each candidate, have the owner give a one-minute brief: What did they observe? What data are they expecting to see? What would confirm or challenge their hunch?
  2. Draft the hypothesis using the three-part structure: What happened (the expected data-supported observation) → Why it matters (the business connection) → What's next (the likely recommendation). Write this down in brief, even in rough language.
  3. Mark explicitly what you're assuming. "This assumes LinkedIn ER data confirms the engagement we observed. If it doesn't, the story changes to..." Naming the assumption makes the data validation step faster.
  4. Rate your confidence: "We're fairly sure about this" vs. "this could go either way depending on the data." Low-confidence hypotheses need the owner to check the data first before the reporting session.
  5. Assign data validation: who will pull the relevant metrics in the first week of next month and confirm or revise the hypothesis?
Example — W4 hypothesis for the Sustainability Campaign

Hypothesis: "We expect LinkedIn engagement data to show ER well above our 3.5% target, driven by the two-part innovation series. If confirmed: our Innovation R&D narrative is gaining traction in the channel where our target audience is most engaged — a sustainable approach to continue in Q3. If media pickup data disappoints: the story shifts to strong social presence but earned media strategy needs adjustment."

Assumption flagged: "This assumes Muck Rack data shows ≥ 50 top-tier mentions. If it shows significantly less, we revise the hypothesis toward a mixed story."

Confidence: High on the social data; medium on the media pickup.

Owner: Channel lead will validate against Sprinklr and Muck Rack data in the first week of June.

Watch out for
  • Treating the hypothesis as a conclusion. The whole value of this step is that it's provisional. If the data doesn't confirm it, the hypothesis changes — and that's fine. That's the system working.
  • Skipping W4 when time is short. This is the ten minutes that saves an hour at the start of next month. It's worth protecting.
  • Vague hypotheses. "We expect good results" isn't testable. A hypothesis names a specific metric, a direction, and what you'd recommend if confirmed.

From Data to Insight

Data → Information → Insights → Action

Four distinct things. Most reporting stops at the second.

When to use this

When you're looking at a set of numbers and trying to figure out what to do with them — or when you've drafted a narrative that feels more like a summary than an insight.

The core idea

These are four distinct stages, not synonyms. Moving from one to the next requires active thought — the chain doesn't happen automatically.

DATA — Raw numbers. "Email CTR: 4.2%"

INFORMATION — Data with context. "Email CTR is 4.2%, 14% below the 4.9% target and down 3% from last month."

INSIGHT — Information with interpretation. "This is the third consecutive month email CTR has underperformed, despite stable open rates — suggesting the subject lines are working but the content isn't motivating action."

ACTION — Insight with a next step. "We need to audit CTA language and format in the last three months of benefits emails and run an A/B test in June before the July enrollment deadline."

The jump from information to insight is the hardest step — it requires asking what the pattern means, not just what it shows. The jump from insight to action requires asking who owns the next move.

How to do it
  1. Start with the data point. State the number and the time period. Resist adding interpretation yet.
  2. Add context: What's the target? What was it last month? What's the trend over time? This moves data to information.
  3. Look for patterns: Is this the first time, or part of a pattern? Is it isolated to one metric or connected to others that are moving in the same direction?
  4. Ask "What does this tell us?" What is the pattern saying about what's working or not working? This is the interpretation step. It often helps to say it out loud before writing it down.
  5. Ask "So what?" What would change if this pattern continued? What opportunity or risk does it represent for the business?
  6. Ask "What should we do?" A specific action, with a named owner and a timeframe. If you can't answer this, the insight isn't complete yet.
Example — Benefits Enrollment email CTR

Data: "Email CTR: 4.2% in May."

Information: "4.2% is 14% below our 4.9% target. It was 4.3% in April and 4.4% in March. Open rates across the same period: 28–29%, stable."

Insight: "Three consecutive months below CTR target, with stable open rates, suggests the subject lines are generating opens but the email content isn't compelling action. This is a content/CTA problem, not a deliverability or subject line problem."

Action: "The FirstUp channel lead will audit the last three months of benefits emails, run an A/B test on CTA language in the June send, and report results before the July enrollment deadline."

Watch out for
  • Presenting information as insight. Adding "this represents a challenge" to a number is editorializing, not interpreting. Real insight names the mechanism — why something is happening, not just that it's good or bad.
  • Skipping to action without the insight step. Acting on data without understanding the pattern leads to treating symptoms instead of causes.
  • Single-period conclusions. One month of underperformance is a watch. Three months is an insight. The pattern is what gives you confidence to act.

From Data to Insight

Reading signals: real insights vs. noise

Not every movement is meaningful. Here's how to tell.

When to use this

When a metric moves significantly in either direction and you're not sure whether to flag it, or when someone asks "is this a good number?" and you need to give a considered answer.

The core idea

Data moves constantly. Most of that movement is noise — natural variance, seasonal patterns, platform algorithm shifts. A real signal is movement that's large enough, sustained enough, and connected enough to be meaningful. Before you call something a signal, it needs to clear three tests: it's big enough to matter, it's happened more than once, and you can connect it to something you did or something that happened.

How to evaluate a potential signal
  1. How large is the change? As a general guide: movement of 10% or more versus target or prior period is worth attention. Movement under 5% is likely noise unless it's a very stable metric where small changes are meaningful.
  2. How sustained is it? One month: watch it. Two months: note it in reporting. Three months: investigate and act. The Benefits Enrollment email CTR pattern is a signal on its third month in a way it wasn't on its first.
  3. Was it expected? A planned campaign driving engagement above target is confirming a success, not a surprise signal. An unexpected drop is worth more attention than an unexpected rise, because unexpected drops often have correctable causes.
  4. Are other related metrics moving in the same direction? Signals are stronger when multiple indicators corroborate. Email CTR down + open rate stable = content problem. Email CTR down + open rate down = deliverability or subject line problem. The combination tells you more than either metric alone.
  5. Is there an external explanation? Seasonal patterns, external events, platform algorithm changes, and competitor activity can all move metrics independently of anything you did. Before calling something a signal about your communications, ask whether the context explains it.
Example — Top Tier Media Coverage

In May, top-tier media coverage dropped 5% month-over-month, to 47 pieces, against a target of 53.

Month 1 (May): This is a watch. One month of decline below target — worth noting in the report, but not a basis for changing strategy. The right call: mention it in the dashboard and include it in the "What to Watch" narrative for the Sustainability story.

If June shows the same: Two months below target. Now it's a note in the executive report — something leadership should be aware of, with an initial hypothesis about why (pitch angle? Story timing? Outlet priorities?).

If July shows the same: Three months. This is now a signal. Investigate: is it pitch volume (T3), pitch quality, story selection, or external factors (media landscape shift)? Act: revise the pitch approach with a specific change, and set a clear checkpoint for results.

Watch out for
  • Over-reacting to one-month movements. Changes strategy in response to single-period data create churn without improving outcomes. Wait for the pattern.
  • Under-reacting to sustained trends. The Benefits email CTR pattern over three months was more concerning than any single month's number — and it required action before the July deadline.
  • Confusing correlation with causation. Two things moving together doesn't mean one caused the other. Town hall attendance went up the same month app active users went up — but the connection needs evidence, not assumption.

Narrative & Communication

Story selection: choosing what to elevate

The executive report can hold 2–3 stories. Here's how to choose.

When to use this

In the early days of Month N+1, once data is available and you're finalizing the Monthly Communications Narrative. You have more story candidates than the report can hold and you need to make editorial choices.

The core idea

The executive report can hold 2–3 stories. Full stop. Choosing what goes in is an editorial decision — not a political one. The criteria should be explicit and consistent so that when someone asks "why did this story make the report but that one didn't?" you can answer clearly.

The report that tells three stories well is more valuable than the report that mentions eight things. Curation is a core skill, not a convenience.

How to do it
  1. List your story candidates with their confirmed data. Apply the four-question test from "What makes a story" to each one.
  2. Score on three criteria: Does it have a clear T1 connection (business impact)? Is there a definitive outcome — strong, mixed, or concerning? Does it connect to a quarterly priority theme? Stories that clear all three have the strongest claim to the executive report.
  3. Check for variety across your selected stories. The best reports include at least one story at each signal level — strong, mixed or watch. A report of three "strong" stories feels like a highlight reel; a report that includes a "watch" builds trust.
  4. Apply the tiebreaker: Which story would be most consequential if leadership didn't know about it? Prioritize the story leadership most needs to hear — not the story that makes the team look best.
  5. Send the rest to the appendix or dashboard. Stories that didn't make the executive report aren't lost — they live in the Tier 2 metrics and the dashboard. They're available if leadership asks.
Example — May story selection

Four candidates after W4: Sustainability Campaign (strong — confirmed by data), Q1 Town Hall Series close (strong), Safety Communications (mixed — coverage below target), Benefits Enrollment (watch — CTR miss for third month).

Common mistake: Selecting Sustainability and Town Hall because they're strong, and noting Safety as a brief "also happening." Benefits doesn't make the report.

Better choice: Sustainability (strong, connects to Q3 Innovation priority), Town Hall Series (strong, connects to Leadership & Culture theme), Benefits Enrollment (watch — leadership needs to know about the CTR trend before the July deadline). The Safety story goes to the dashboard; the trend isn't definitive enough yet.

The better choice includes the uncomfortable story because it's time-sensitive and decision-relevant. That's what builds trust with leadership over time.

Watch out for
  • Selecting stories that make the team look good rather than stories that inform leadership. Long-term credibility comes from honest reporting — not from month-over-month good news management.
  • Avoiding watch or red stories. If leadership discovers a problem through another channel before the comms report surfaces it, trust erodes quickly.
  • Choosing based on available data rather than importance. Sometimes the most important story is the one where data is incomplete. A hypothesis with caveats can be more useful than a complete data set for an unimportant metric.

Narrative & Communication

Crafting a compelling narrative

The three-part structure that turns data into a story leadership can act on.

When to use this

When you're writing the story tiles for the monthly executive report — or any time a stakeholder asks "what's the story here?" and you need to turn a set of metrics into a brief, clear narrative.

The core idea

Every strong communications narrative answers three questions, in order: What happened? (Specific. Data-grounded. No preamble.) Why does it matter? (Connected to a business priority. Says something that isn't obvious from the data alone.) What's next? (A concrete, owned action — or a clear question for leadership.)

Each part is one to two sentences. The whole tile is 60–90 words. If it takes longer to say, it hasn't been distilled enough.

How to do it
  1. Write "What happened" first — and make it specific. Lead with the metric, the movement, and the context against target or prior period. No scene-setting, no background. The observation first.Weak: "The Sustainability Innovation Campaign performed well this month with strong social metrics."
    Strong: "LinkedIn's two-part innovation series drove a 22% engagement rate in May — 19% above target and the highest-performing content month since October 2025."
  2. Write "Why it matters" second — and connect it to the business. This is the hardest part. It requires knowing what leadership actually cares about and saying something that goes beyond restating the data.Weak: "This shows our content strategy is working."
    Strong: "Sustained narrative presence in the Innovation theme builds the brand authority that informs purchasing decisions and supports the trust relationships the commercial team depends on. This continues a six-month upward trend — one of three Q3 priorities."
  3. Write "What's next" last — and make it actionable. One specific action. A named owner implied. A timeframe or trigger.Weak: "We will continue to invest in LinkedIn content."
    Strong: "We'll extend the LinkedIn series into Q3 and adjust the media pitch angle toward third-party validation data to close the gap on top-tier coverage, which came in 12% below target."
  4. Read it back at speed. If you can't get through it in 30 seconds, it's too long. Cut anything that isn't carrying its weight.
Full example — Sustainability Innovation Campaign

What happened: LinkedIn's two-part innovation series drove a 22% engagement rate in May — 19% above target and the highest-performing content month since October 2025. Priority narrative presence in top-tier coverage reached 74%, a new high and the fourth consecutive month of improvement.

Why it matters: Sustained narrative presence in the Innovation, R&D & AI theme builds the brand authority that protects and differentiates 3M in markets where competitors are increasingly active. The six-month trend confirms this as one of our most productive strategic narratives.

What's next: Continue the LinkedIn series in June. Revise the media pitch angle toward third-party data validation to address the top-tier coverage gap, which came in 12% below target despite strong pitch volume.

Watch out for
  • Leading with metrics instead of outcomes. "Social ER was 3.8%" as an opener buries the story. Start with what the metric means, not what it is.
  • Communications jargon in leadership-facing narratives. "Earned media share of voice" and "organic engagement rate" need plain-language companions for non-specialists. Or just use the plain language version.
  • "We will continue to monitor." This is not a "What's next." It's a statement of inaction. Replace it with a specific action or a named question for leadership to weigh in on.

Writing for leadership

The principles that make the difference between a report that gets read and one that gets skimmed.

When to use this

When finalizing the monthly executive report — or any communication directed at the CMO, CCO, or other senior stakeholders. Also when connecting a communications outcome to its business significance (the "why it matters" step).

The core idea

Leadership reads quickly and alongside many competing inputs. They're not there to learn about what the comms team did — they're there to understand what's happening and what, if anything, requires their attention or decision. The narrative has about thirty seconds to earn its place in their attention. Everything in the report should be in service of that constraint.

Seven principles for writing that lands
  1. Lead with the "so what," not the context. The most important thing goes first. Background, methodology, and caveats go later — or not at all.
  2. Write shorter than feels comfortable. If a sentence can be cut without losing meaning, cut it. Senior readers interpret unnecessary length as a lack of editorial judgment.
  3. Avoid hedging language. "May suggest," "could indicate," and "appears to" undermine confidence in the analysis. Say what you mean: "This indicates X" or "We believe X because Y."
  4. Never make leadership do math. "Social ER was 3.8%, 19% above our 3.2% target" is better than "Social ER was 3.8% against a 3.2% target." Do the comparison for them — it signals that you've done the thinking.
  5. One action per story. If you have three recommendations, you haven't identified the most important one yet. Pick it.
  6. Connect to what they already know. Reference the quarterly priorities, the challenges they've named, the metrics they've asked about before. This signals that the reporting team is paying attention.
  7. Be honest about what you don't know. "This is the third consecutive month below target — we are investigating and will have a recommendation by [date]" is more credible than smoothing over a pattern leadership might already be aware of. Trust is built by naming problems, not by appearing to not have them.
Example — the same story, two versions

Version A (buries the lede): "The Benefits Enrollment push has been an active communications effort throughout Q2, involving multiple email sends to the full employee base, an app notification campaign, and manager communication guides. In May, we sent 14 emails with an average open rate of 28.4%, which is in line with our historical average. However, CTR performance came in at 4.2%, which was below our 4.9% target."

Version B (leads with the so what): "Internal Benefits Enrollment email CTR has missed its 4.9% target for three consecutive months, despite stable open rates — indicating the content isn't driving action, not a deliverability problem. With the July enrollment deadline approaching, we're running a June A/B test on CTA language; results by July 1."

Version B is shorter, more honest, and more useful. It tells leadership what they need to know, why it matters, and what's being done — in that order.

Watch out for
  • Over-explaining methodology. Leadership trusts you to be rigorous. They don't need to see how the numbers were derived — only the conclusion and what it implies.
  • Writing the narrative that makes the team look best. The narrative that's most useful to leadership is what builds long-term trust — even when it includes uncomfortable information.
  • Confusing "comprehensive" with "thorough." A comprehensive report covers everything. A thorough report covers the right things with depth and precision. Thorough is what leadership needs.

Recommendations that drive action

Why insights often don't lead to action — and the four things that change that.

When to use this

When writing the "What's next" section of a story tile, or when preparing to present an insight that requires a decision or behavior change from someone else.

The core idea

Insights often don't lead to action — not because people don't care, but because the recommendation is too vague, too numerous, or not owned by anyone. A recommendation that drives action has four components: a specific action (not a direction), a named owner, a timeframe, and — where relevant — the consequence if nothing changes.

One well-formed recommendation is worth more than a list of five weak ones.

How to do it
  1. State the action specifically. "Revise CTA language in benefits emails" — not "improve email performance." The specific action is something a person can do on a Tuesday morning. The direction is something they can ignore.
  2. Name the owner. "The FirstUp channel lead will..." — not "the team should..." An unowned action has a very low probability of being completed.
  3. Set a timeframe or trigger. "Before the June send" — not "in the coming weeks." Time-bound or event-bound actions are more likely to happen than open-ended ones.
  4. State the consequence of inaction, where relevant. "If June CTR doesn't improve, we'll miss the enrollment window before the July deadline." This isn't alarmism — it's honest prioritization. It signals to leadership what's at stake.
  5. Limit to one recommendation per story tile. If you have three, you either have three separate stories or you haven't yet identified the most important action.
  6. Separate what the team can act on from what requires leadership input. Some actions are within the team's authority; others require a resourcing decision or a strategic choice. Be explicit about which is which.
Example — weak vs. strong recommendation

Weak: "We should consider ways to improve our media outreach strategy to drive more top-tier coverage." Nobody owns this. Nothing will happen.

Strong: "The media relations lead will revise the Sustainability pitch to emphasize third-party validation data and test with two tier-1 target outlets before the June 15 deadline. If coverage doesn't improve by month-end, we'll reassess the story angle for Q3." Specific, owned, time-bound, with a built-in checkpoint.

Watch out for
  • "We will continue to monitor." This is not a recommendation — it's a statement that nothing is changing. Every "monitor" recommendation should become a "we'll monitor and act if X by Y."
  • Too many recommendations. Three recommendations at the end of an exec report means none of them feel urgent. One clear recommendation with an owner is more likely to get done than three without.
  • Recommendations without ownership. If a recommendation doesn't have a person's name (or role) attached to it, put it back in draft until it does.

Using AI as a thinking partner

Where AI genuinely helps — and where your judgment is irreplaceable.

When to use this

When you're stuck interpreting what a set of metrics means, when you can't find the "why it matters" for a story, or when you need a first draft of a story tile before you refine it into something accurate and specific.

The core idea

AI tools are most useful at two points in the reporting process: helping you move from data to interpretation (the hardest step), and generating a first draft that you refine rather than writing from scratch. They're not a replacement for your judgment — they don't know your organization's context, your leadership's priorities, or what's actually true about a story. Think of AI as a fast, available thinking partner: good at generating options, not good at knowing which option is right.

Where AI helps most
  1. Getting unstuck on interpretation. Paste three months of metric data and your target, then ask: "What patterns do you see, and what are three possible explanations for each?" AI can surface interpretations you might not have considered — you evaluate which one actually fits your context.
  2. Drafting the "why it matters." This is the hardest sentence to write. Try: "Here's what happened [paste observation]. Here's our Q3 priority theme. Help me write two or three versions of one to two sentences connecting this outcome to why leadership should care." Pick the best one and make it specific to your situation.
  3. Generating a first draft story tile. Give AI the data, the context, and the three-part structure (What happened / Why it matters / What's next). It will produce a draft. Your job is to revise it for accuracy, specificity, and organizational context — the things AI doesn't know.
  4. Checking your narrative. Paste a finished story tile and ask: "Does this lead with outcomes or with activity? Is the 'why it matters' specific or generic? What's missing?" Useful for a fast editorial check before submitting.
How to prompt effectively
  1. Give context: Tell the AI your role, your audience, and the goal of the communication. "I'm a communications manager writing a story tile for a monthly executive report. The audience is the CCO and two SVPs."
  2. Give the data explicitly. Paste the actual numbers, not a paraphrase. AI works better with specifics.
  3. Give the strategic context. Mention the quarterly theme, the priority this story connects to, and any relevant background the AI wouldn't know.
  4. Ask for options, not a single answer. "Give me three versions of the 'why it matters' sentence, ranging from conservative to bold" gives you more to work with than asking for one answer.
  5. Review critically. AI sounds confident even when it's wrong or generic. You are the person who knows whether the interpretation is actually true. Don't publish a draft you haven't verified against the actual data and organizational context.
Example prompt — getting unstuck on "why it matters"

What you'd share with AI:

"I'm writing a story tile for a corporate communications executive report. Here's what happened: our LinkedIn engagement rate for Innovation-themed content was 3.8% in May, 19% above our 3.2% target. This is the fourth consecutive month of above-target performance. Our Q3 priority theme is 'Innovation, R&D & AI' — we're trying to establish 3M as a credible voice in the AI and sustainable innovation space. Write me three versions of one to two sentences explaining why this result matters to the CCO."

What you'd do with AI's response: Read the three options, identify what rings true, discard the generic language, and rewrite the best version to include the specific nuance you know is accurate. AI gets you from zero to sixty; your knowledge gets you from sixty to a hundred.

Watch out for
  • Using AI output without fact-checking it. AI doesn't know what actually happened in your organization. It will produce plausible-sounding text that may be factually wrong about your specific situation.
  • Accepting the first draft. AI first drafts sound polished but are often generic. Your revision is what makes it specific, accurate, and credible to an audience that knows the context.
  • Using AI to generate the insight rather than to articulate one you've already formed. The interpretation — what the data means and why it matters — is your job. AI helps you express it more clearly; it can't form it for you.
The rule of thumb

If you can explain the insight in your own words before you prompt AI, the AI output will be useful. If you can't, you're not ready to use AI yet — you need to do more thinking first.
